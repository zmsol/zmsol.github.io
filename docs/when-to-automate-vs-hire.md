# When to Automate vs. When to Hire: A Decision Framework

**The wrong call costs you either wasted tool spend or a salary you didn't need to pay.**

Every growing professional services firm hits this crossroads: do I buy a tool or hire a person? The software vendor says automate everything. The partner who wants headcount says hire. Both have a bias. This framework removes it.

---

## The One Number That Decides

**If a task takes your team more than 10 hours per week total, it justifies a dedicated tool or system.**

That's the threshold. Here's the math:

| Weekly Hours on Task | Annual Cost at $75/hr (blended staff cost) | Cost to Automate (One-Time) | Breakeven |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------------|---------------------------|-----------|
| 5 hrs/wk | $19,500 | $3,000–6,000 | 2–4 months |
| 10 hrs/wk | $39,000 | $5,000–8,000 | 2–3 months |
| 20 hrs/wk | $78,000 | $8,000–15,000 | 2–3 months |
| 40 hrs/wk (1 FTE) | $156,000 | $10,000–20,000 | 1–2 months |

**Automation wins on any task above 5 hrs/wk.** Below that, a process improvement or template might be enough.

## The Three-Question Test

Before you decide, answer these three questions about the task:

### 1. Is the input structured or predictable?

Can you describe the inputs in advance? Client fills a form, email comes in with specific fields, data arrives in a spreadsheet?

- **Yes** → Good candidate for automation
- **No, it varies wildly each time** → Keep human in the loop

### 2. Does the output need to be exactly right every time?

Is this a compliance report, an invoice, or a client-facing document where errors cost you money?

- **Yes** → Automate the data aggregation, keep human review for judgment calls
- **No, it's internal only** → Full automation is fine

### 3. Would a junior person be doing this in 6 months anyway?

If the task needs to exist as a role, it's a hire. If it's a repetitive process that could be eliminated entirely once built, it's automation.

- **It's a permanent need that requires judgment** → Hire
- **It's a repetitive process** → Automate
- **It's a recurring need that requires some of both** → Automate the repetitive parts, reassign the human to the judgment parts

## The Framework in Practice

### Scenario A: Monthly Compliance Reporting

**Before:** A compliance officer spends 3 full days pulling data from 4 systems, formatting it, and producing a report.

- Input: Structured (data lives in systems)
- Output: Needs to be exact (audit-ready)
- Long-term: Filing requirement, not a role

**Call:** Automate data collection and report generation. Keep the officer for analysis and review. Result: 3 days → 90 minutes.

### Scenario B: Client Intake and Onboarding

**Before:** An admin spends 8 hrs/wk emailing clients, collecting forms, setting up files, and sending confirmations.

- Input: Structured (forms, emails)
- Output: Needs to be correct (client-facing)
- Long-term: Scales with client count

**Call:** Automate the intake sequence, document generation, and file setup. Keep the admin for exceptions and relationship management. Result: 8 hrs → 1 hr.

### Scenario C: Custom Strategy Documents

**Before:** A senior consultant spends 6 hours writing each strategy document from scratch.

- Input: Unpredictable (client-specific)
- Output: Needs judgment (strategic)
- Long-term: Core service

**Call:** Don't automate the thinking. Do build templates and frameworks that cut drafting time by 60%. Result: 6 hrs → 2.5 hrs.

## A Note on the Human Cost

Hiring adds overhead beyond salary: benefits, training, management time, office space, turnover risk. The fully loaded cost of a $55K admin hire is closer to $75–80K. The fully loaded cost of a $5K automation project is $5K plus a small monthly maintenance fee.

**Automation doesn't replace people. It replaces the work that shouldn't be done by people.** The team member who was doing 15 hrs/wk of manual data entry gets reassigned to the client work they were hired for.
